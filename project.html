Discovery

Problem:

Users need a photo storage program that is simple to use, works across platforms, and makes sharing possible to anyone.

Solution:

Keeps is a platform that was created with all users in mind. It shares and organizes photos and videos easily, and in ways that any user could understand.

Research

Competitive Analysis:

The first step of my research was to analyze what other cloud storage programs were out there. I chose Google Drive, Dropbox, and Pinterest for this process and sorted through their strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats in a SWOT Analysis.

Through this exercise I was able to pinpoint some holes in the marketplace that my cloud storage program could exploit. The main takeaways were that these programs were too complex and would not appeal to users who didn’t use technology often. The other was that there was no program that is visually appealing, while staying out of the “social networking” category

  • + Real time collaboration
  • - Can easily get unorganized
  • + Syncs easily to your phone
  • - Sharing is complicated
  • + Aesthetically pleasing
  • - Over-saturated with content

User Survey

After gaining my own insights on the competitors, I created a survey to gain an insight into how the general public felt about cloud storage programs. The survey was taken by users from all over the country (a few international as well) and many varying ages and occupations. This variance was very helpful in determining that the answers weren’t just a result of a certain demographic. Among those who use cloud storage programs already, the major takeaways were that they mainly use it for personal reasons, they want it to be less complicated, and they need to be able to upload photos from their device and collaborate with others. For those who do not use a cloud storage program, the overwhelming majority do not use it because they are too complicated, but they would be open to a more simple program in the future. There seems to be a big gap in the market here for users who would use the features of a cloud storage program, but do not want to deal with all of the complexities of those currently in use.

    • Easily\derailed by the current cloud storage programs

Information Architecture

User Stories:

Based on the user personas and keeping in mind that Keeps had to be a simple program, I created user stories in order to identify which features would be most important to include. The most important features that I landed on for returning users were:

  • I want to upload content
  • I want to share a single item with someone else
  • I want to share a folder or group of items with someone else
  • I want to connect with other users for real-time collaboration in notes or documents
  • I want to log in to my account

Sketches and Wireframes

I began putting my research and branding into action with my wireframes. I started with sketching the screens that would accurately depict what was drawn up in the user flows. These sketches were then developed into lo-fi wireframes. From these wireframes I created a clickable prototype and had the first round of usability testing. This testing was crucial to influencing my final designs, and ultimately resulted in two major changes. The complete removal of the “share link” function of the program as testers deemed it unnecessary, and taking the “about” section off the homepage because it was information overload.

Branding

Name:

The name appeared after I realized the word “Keeps” continuously appeared in my analyses and mind mapping exercises early in my research. By being a short and simple word, it sticks with the themes I want the program to adhere to, and the word goes well with a cloud program and can be easily used for slogans. Examples would be: “Keeps all of your content in one place” “Keeps your content secure” “Keeps cloud storage simple.” Originally the name had been “Keyps” but since this is a program all about simplicity, the original words seemed more fitting.

Logo:

The logo came about after many variations and concepts were explored. Different versions of keys, locks, save icons (or floppy disks as I still see them...), and plays on the letter K were created. Eventually the key was what came up the most, and the simplicity combined with the similarity to the name became the deciding factor. Originally, the key itself had two lines coming out from it to create a “K” shape. While it made the logo more memorable, it was quite busy which detracted from the simple brand image that is meant to be portrayed.

Color:

While there is plenty of white space to highlight simplicity, the other bright colors were used as to demonstrate that Keeps is targeted for personal use and not professional use. The main color for the brand is a green/blue. Green and blue are both colors that are perceived to be comforting and soothing, as Keeps is designed to be a solution for complicated overly professional cloud platforms. This color is also light and not very strong, this combined with the second yellow color which depicts happiness, also helps to demonstrate that it is meant to be fun and personal rather than formal.

Conclusions

I grew and learned a lot as a designer from creating Keeps from start to finish:

Every stage in the design process is important, there are no shortcuts. If I ever felt that I didn’t need a step or I would be fine pushing ahead, it always resulted in going back and having to redo a lot of my work. Learning to trust the process was difficult for me but once I finally figured it out the hard way, it made me a much more efficient designer.

Outside opinions are crucial. I never would have been happy with my final result if I didn’t conduct testing or have other designers give their thoughts. Tunnel vision became very real for me on this project and I am very thankful I was able to harness this input so effectively into my final design.

Mistakes are okay and neccessary. Looking back through the stages of this project, the design came a long way from where I started. The evolution of the design and iterations are needed for a successful final project. Trial and error will never hurt you when it comes to design.
